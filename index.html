About Me

I didn’t come to Computer Science through machines. I came through people—through trying to understand how we think, how we make meaning, how we try to connect.

I started in economics and behavioral science, drawn to the patterns behind human behavior. But theory only takes you so far. At some point, I wanted tools—something I could build with, test ideas through, and learn from in the real world.

That search led me to Computer Science, and eventually to Natural Language Processing. It felt like the right intersection: structure and ambiguity, systems and language. A space where you can model behavior—but also listen to it.

I’m also a parent, which sharpens the stakes. I think often about the world my son will inherit—not just the tools we pass down, but the assumptions built into them. I want to be part of shaping systems that remain aware of the people inside them. That leave space for complexity. That don’t mistake efficiency for care.

I’m still learning what that takes. But that’s the kind of work I want to do.
