Ambition and Goals

Looking back, I think I’ve suffered more from ambition than from lacking it. The periods when I pushed hardest—testing limits, chasing goals—were often my most miserable and least creative. Goal-driven effort helped up to a point. But once the stakes rose and the path grew hazy, goals stopped guiding and began to interfere. They pulled my attention toward an imagined future, while the real difficulty sat much closer to home: I didn’t yet understand what I was actually suited to do next.

That tension led me to reconsider what goals are meant to serve. Beneath them sits ambition. And ambition, for me, isn’t about maximizing output or accumulating achievements. It’s about tending to conditions—clarity of mind, steadiness of attention, enough energy to think well. When those are missing, even sensible goals begin to feel beside the point.

Part of the confusion is how easily ambition slips into busyness, or into comparison. It’s tempting to believe that pressure sharpens us, that a little self-criticism will do the work patience won’t. But this assumes that we function best under force, and that our worth becomes clearer when placed on a scale. I’m no longer sure either is true.

I think of something Jane Goodall once said—that each person has a role to play, even if they never quite name it. What stays with me isn’t the reassurance, but the orientation. It shifts the question away from effort and toward attention: not how do I push harder? but what draws me, even when I’m not forcing it?

That question doesn’t respond well to force. We talk about passion as though it were a choice, but it rarely behaves like one. You don’t choose who you fall in love with. You can’t will yourself into genuine fascination. Whatever you’re most suited to already exerts a quiet pull. The difficulty is learning how to listen.

This may be why solitude has mattered to writers and thinkers for so long. Distance creates room for self-observation. Hardship may be unavoidable, but self-knowledge seems to arrive more readily when inner friction is eased rather than intensified.

Jane Austen’s life illustrates this. During years of instability, she wrote very little. Only when her circumstances settled did the work we now admire begin to appear. Ease, in her case, wasn’t indulgence. It was necessity.

When goals begin to feel obstructive, it may be because they point outward too soon. Before deciding where to go, it helps to notice what is already there.
