On Sisyphus

Oh: We has a rule. Nine mistakes and you are out.
Tip: How many mistakes do you have?
Oh: Sixty-two.

— Home (2015)

1. A Different Kind of Failure

The first time I heard the story of Sisyphus—the man cursed to roll a boulder uphill forever, only to watch it fall—I saw it as punishment. A warning to the proud. Endless effort, no reward. A loop designed not just to tire the body, but to break the spirit.

But the image lingered—not for its grandeur, but for its quiet ache. This wasn’t a dramatic fall from grace. It was something quieter, more unsettling: a failure that wasn't sudden or final, just endless.

What do you do with effort that leads nowhere?
What does it mean to keep trying when you know how it ends?

2. The Tyranny of Outcomes

Keynes once wrote, “In the long run, we are all dead.” He wasn’t joking. He was criticizing those who dismissed present pain in favor of some distant fix.

But the line stays with me in another way. If everything ends—if the summit always resets—why strive at all? Why chase goals when death eventually wipes the slate clean?

It sounds bleak. But maybe it’s just the wrong lens.

Brushing your teeth doesn’t work once. Neither do friendships. Or marriage. Or art. These things matter not because they’re finished, but because they’re tended. Again and again.

3. Mistakes That Teach

An economics professor once told me: “Rationality means not making the same mistake twice.” It sounded neat. Clean. Reasonable.

But what is the same mistake?

Things change. You change. Two failures might look alike on the outside, but inside, they’re different. A new perspective. A new question. Sometimes the only way to know what’s changed is to do it again.

Yet the quote stuck because it surfaces something we rarely admit: we fear repetition. We crave resolution. We want to get it right the first time.

But most learning is circling back. Love. Skill. Insight. None of it moves in straight lines.

4. The Work That Repeats

Deep work rarely arrives in one stroke. A proof doesn’t land whole. A poem isn’t born finished. Writers, builders, and thinkers return to the same place—not because they’re lost, but because they’re still listening.

The more something matters, the more it pulls you back. Not to repeat—but to re-meet. To see what you missed. To notice what’s shifted in you.

Even procrastination looks different in this light. Some research suggests stepping away lets your mind quietly reorganize. What feels like delay might be slow progress in disguise.

Annie Duke puts it plainly: you can’t judge a decision by its outcome. Bad luck can follow a good choice. Good luck can hide a bad one.

If we care about growth—not just success—then the test is how we return. How we sit with the hard parts. How we keep going, eyes open.

5. Time, Tinkering, and Learning to See

Physicist Leonard Mlodinow once noted that Stephen Hawking worked as if he had all the time in the world.

It wasn’t just patience. It was trust in the process—an understanding that real insight unfolds slowly. What looks like failure might be a shift in how you see. What looks like repetition might be refinement.

Most creative work isn’t a march to the finish. It’s a quiet kind of tinkering. A nudge, a return, another try.

And people grow that way, too. You revisit the same question, the same fear—not because you failed to solve it, but because you’re someone new each time you try.

6. The Point Was Never the Summit

Maybe Sisyphus wasn’t being punished. Maybe he was practicing—meeting the same weight, the same slope, each time with a slightly different mind.

The rock didn’t change. But he did.

Edison said, “I haven’t failed. I’ve just found 10,000 ways that won’t work.” We call it a cliché now, but it holds. If all you track is the result, then yes—it’s failure. But Edison wasn’t counting wins. He was learning the shape of the problem.

Each "no" was information.

Sisyphus was condemned for pride, trickery, defiance. The gods gave him a task that unraveled the moment he completed it. But maybe the real trap wasn’t the rock. Maybe it was the belief that the goal was to win.

He didn’t fail because the rock fell. He only failed if he thought keeping it up was the point.

The rock never stayed.
But it taught him how to see.
